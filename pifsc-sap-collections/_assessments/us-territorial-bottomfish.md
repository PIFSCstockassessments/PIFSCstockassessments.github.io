The U.S. territorial bottomfish complexes are composed of 14 unique bottomfish species, of which, 11 are found in American Samoa, and 13 in both the Commonwealth of the Northern Mariana Islands (CNMI) and Guam. The complex includes species from the snapper, emperor, grouper, and jack families. In each of the three territories, fishing is an integral part of the heritage, tradition, and culture of the local communities. 

Most bottomfishing has traditionally been done with hook-and-line from small boats with outboard engines, primarily for subsistence or local sale. In recent years, technology and the increased presence of larger vessels have allowed the fisheries to expand offshore into deeper waters around the islands. The complex is managed as one, multi-species complex around each territory by the Western Pacific Regional Fishery Management Council.

The most recent benchmark assessment was developed in 2019 and used a Bayesian surplus production model that fit annual biomass estimates to estimates of total catch and catch-per-unit-effort (CPUE) data for each territory. In the 2019 benchmark assessment, the modeling software [JABBA](/packages/JABBA/) was implemented and new information was incorporated into the model. Commercial fishing sales receipts and fishery-dependent survey data were used to inform catch estimates and abundance trends. 

The PIFSC Stock Assessment Program is currently working on the next cutting-edge stock assessments for each Territorial Bottomfish complex, due to the Western Pacific Fisheries Management Council in 2023-2025. These assessments will incorporate ongoing scientific research, fisheries surveys, catch sampling, and advanced stock modeling approaches to best support the sustainable management of these important fisheries.
